Abstract

Parental involvement in homework is a pivotal high stakes domestic activity resulting in complex emotional dynamics. While prior work established behavioral taxonomy through frequency based counts, hidden structural logic governing conflict escalation and resilience remains a blind spot. To uncover these patterns, we propose Struct-ENA, a multi level structural modeling framework that deconstructs 475 hours of naturalistic parent and child interactions. Our analysis reveals that domestic interactions are not organized by simple emotional valence but by a functional backbone where a central directive hub serves as the common source for supportive reinforcement and negative frustration. We identify a fundamental structural bifurcation between a constructive instructional loop and a reactive conflict spiral, pinpointing communication conflict as the universal nexus through which specific disagreements are exacerbated. Beyond descriptive analysis, we operationalize these structural topologies as computational blueprints for generative AI. By treating ENA networks as probabilistic state machines, we achieve a form of intelligence degradation that allows large language models to bypass their default rationality bias and authentically simulate non rational behaviors such as nagging loops and emotional triggers. This research provides a computational bridge for the development of high fidelity parental training environments. Our contributions are available at https://struct-ena.github.io/struct-ena/.

Figure 1: A conceptual illustration of our analytical approach. The left side represents the established frequency-based analysis of interactional components, showing the average occurrence of behaviors per session. Building upon this foundation, the right side illustrates our structural approach using Epistemic network analysis (ENA), which reveals the deep coupling and temporal evolution between behaviors (e.g., Guided Inquiry (GI)) and conflict types (e.g., Knowledge Conflict (KC)). The network analysis captures how interactions unfold over time, highlighting two distinct paths: a Resilient Trajectory (blue dashed line) that successfully recovers toward supportive pedagogical nodes, and an Escalation Trajectory (red dashed line) where the family becomes trapped in a negative cycle of conflict.

Figure 2: The ENA network of parental behaviors (RQ1a). A detailed examination of the network reveals a key structural insight: parental guidance is not organized by a simple positive-versus-negative valence. Instead, the structure is built around a functional backbone of instructional and directive strategies, with both positive and negative affective behaviors entangled within these core patterns.

Figure 3: he ENA network of parent-child conflicts (RQ1b).This structure reveals that Communication Conflict is not merely one conflict among many, but a central pathway through which specific disagreements are expressed and potentially inflamed.

Figure 4: The combined ENA model of all behavior and conflict codes, illustrating the structural differences between low-conflict (red dots) and high-conflict (blue dots) families. The horizontal axis (SVD1) separates instruction-focused interactions (left) from control- and emotion-focused interactions (right). The clear spatial separation of the two groups reveals that low-conflict families are structurally characterized by instructional behaviors (e.g., GI, IT), while high-conflict families are characterized by a dense co-occurrence of DC, negative affect (CB, II), and various conflict types.
